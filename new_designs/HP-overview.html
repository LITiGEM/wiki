Our philosophy

Why we did HP the way we did it

Our team developed applications for biological light switches that by controlling specific cellular processes can innovate areas of medicine, illumination and architecture.

We saw the Human Practices activities as opportunities to explore the applications of these biological light switches and develop a holistic perspective on them. In doing so, we tested activities and principles to see how well they work and combined them with the wet lab experiments, mathematical modelling and entrepreneurship. Examples include gamification, small and large scale events, online competitions and engaging with architects.

We hope that our achievements this summer will inspire a more creative view of the role of Human Practices in synthetic biology and will serve as good documentation for future iGEM teams to build upon.

INTEGRATED PRACTICES

What will the future of illumination look like?

Through our Integrated Human Practices activities, we wanted to test a few principles that future teams can use when exploring their projects. We focused on:

  • high outreach
  • interactivity
  • the extent to which we integrate an outcome into the project holistically

We designed our Instagram competition to use these principles to understand the complexity of deploying a bacterial light bulb in the real life. At the end, we reached 78,000 people, received 133 design submissions and developed our final prototype.

Collaborations & Conversations

We saw stakeholder engagement as the opportunity to stress test ideas, engage in a bidirectional exchange of ideas and meet interesting people. Some of our conversations included:

  • Architects
  • Designers
  • Construction Companies
  • Transport for London
  • European Space Agency

UK iGEM Meet-up

At the UK iGEM 2017 meet-up, we tested a tool to use when choosing an iGEM project and developing ideas: debates.

By organising debates on synthetic biology questions, we observed how useful this technique can be to reveal answers and produce more questions. Future iGEM teams can study our observations about the process and build upon it by exploring how debates can be applied to creating chances for feedback on ideas.

PUBLIC ENGAGEMENT

Light can excite cells. Can LIT do the same?

Thought experiment: what will the future of science communication look like? We explored this question through gamification.

Our aim was to test how gamification can be used to communicate science better. We gamified the central dogma of biology and presented the installation at the London Science Museum Late event, with an exposure to 2000 guests.

Check out how it works, reflections and tips for future iGEM teams.

Smart Dinner

Public engagement can happen on small, medium and large scales in terms of the public outreach. We wanted to test each level and see what useful outcomes can be expected from each.

On the small scale, we decided to focus on the idea of intimacy: how can a small-scale discussion facilitate a more thorough understanding of synthetic biology and building close connections with the people involved. We reached to Smart Dinners, who popularly organise dinners where people discuss and debate various themes. The outcomes were quite different from the large-scale events that we held.

Wellcome Trust event

While the first small-scale event was about a free discussion on the topic, we experimented with a more focused conversation to compare the two approaches.

Again, people invited to the event came from different backgrounds and targeted the discussion to policy development around synthetic biology.

Facilitating discussion in synthetic biology

On the medium-scale outreach level, we sought an opportunity to engage with high-school students. The aim was to test the efficacy of discussion facilitation as a technique in synthetic biology.

We used knowledge and techniques from management consulting to engage with students. The result? A very in-depth conversation about how to genetically modify mules to make love to each other – along the way we touched on relevant points on using gene drives, consequences of genetic engineering and real-life trade-offs.
