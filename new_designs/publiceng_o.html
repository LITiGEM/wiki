Where is science communication headed?

When iGEM started, we asked ourselves a question: as an emerging field and not void of controversies, how will synthetic biology engage with people in the future?

The initial engagement is the very first step in science communication, having multiple potential functions: to familiarize, excite, connect people and ideas. We decided to focus on its potential to familiarize people with synthetic biology and excite them about how they could contribute.

We developed an idea that games will play a much bigger role in communicating science in the future and tested our assumptions by observing public perception of such an attempt. We gamified the central dogma of molecular biology at London Science Museumâ€™s Late event, attended by more than 5000 people.

Our installation makes participants take the role of E. Coli cells, learning about the building blocks of any biological system. We combined Dance Dance Revolution with transcription and turned translation into an arcade game.

Facilitating discussion in synthetic biology

We tested the technique of discussion facilitation in a medium-scale event. Based on a team memberâ€™s experience with the guidelines used in management consulting by companies such as Deloitte, we organised the session as an introduction to synthetic biology and our biological light switches and their applications. The lucky ones were adolescents taking part in the UCL Sutton Trust Biosciences Summer School.

Our introduction of the project and synthetic biology focused on asking students what their knowledge about the field is and then built upon this. For example, we highlighted the need for interdisciplinary work and then asked why that might be important. We discussed examples of how mathematical modelling can be combined with wet lab experiments and then entrepreneurship.

What worked well was having the students diverge and talk among themselves about given points and then converge back to share everyoneâ€™s opinion and then summarise conclusion or take-away points along the way.

The most interesting example came in a discussion initiated by a student: â€˜We should make mules fertile, as it can benefit agricultureâ€™.

We went through 4 or 5 turns of diverging and converging to cover points such as:

  • - what are the real-life trade-offs of modifying mules in an agricultural context?
  • - if gene drives are used, what unwanted consequences can come along?
  • how should experimentation happen â€“ behind closed doors or in a transparent manner

What we hope to have had achieved was to give students a more holistic way of looking at ideas in synthetic biology. We encourage future teams to try and seek communication techniques from other fields that specialize in getting to the heart of problems, such as in management consulting.

Using light to guide cell behaviour: exploring societal impacts of technology of the future

We extended the exploration of small-scale events by now focusing the discussion around a specific topic regarding our project: policy. At the Wellcome Trust Reading room, we structured a discussion around the societal and ethical impacts of light-induced technologies with members of various backgrounds.

We proposed that the discussion focused on the limits to using biological light switches in controlling genetically modified organisms and in printing organs.

The outcomes

The outcomes of the discussion can be turned into relevant policies to be developed (future iGEM teams can build upon this by contacting the relevant bodies that regulate these developments):

  • How are we resilient in the face of contamination/ How do we redress?
  • What is the eligibility for using 3D printed organs?
  • How can policy prevent a socio-economic division? What are the practical considerations in terms of costs?

Smart Dinner

We wanted to explore public engagement in a more intimate and closed setting, where we would have enough time to dedicate to talking about synthetic biology and our project.

We found Smart Dinners, a weekly event held in London, where between 8-10 people attend and have a meal, discussing various topics. After getting in touch, we picked a week to talk about synthetic biology and engage with the participants.

Future iGEM teams might want to explore organising small events that while not reaching many people, builds useful connections and relationships with members of the public.
