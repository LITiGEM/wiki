Where is science communication headed?

We started with one thought: people simply love playing, so games should successfully improve science communication and spark interest in synthetic biology.

To test our idea, we designed an installation to provide an intuitive understanding of the central dogma of molecular biology and provoke thought about our project and the future of synthetic biology.

We focused on these outcomes as they seem to be skills that people can use when encountering a new story about recent developments in biology.

How does it work?

The installation involves two participants ‘becoming’ E. Coli cells to learn about the building blocks of any biological system. Put simply, the game will provide an intuitive understanding of how DNA codes for proteins.

We chose to combine the DANCE DANCE Revolution game with biology. Conceptually, the first person performs transcription of DNA to mRNA, while the second participant does the translation into protein.

Reflections & considerations

We saw people’s familiarity with video dance games as an exciting area to explore, as this immediately translates to a quick and intense experience. The aim was to at least spark interest in the biology behind our project.

Future iGEM teams could try to think about activities/areas that initially might seem not to have any link to synthetic biology, but which can turn into platforms for communicating scientific concepts.
