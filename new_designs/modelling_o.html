LEGIT (Light Expressed Gene Induced Transport)

LEGIT outlines the steps involved in the light activation of E.coli cells to create 3D structures. We wanted to ensure LIT could create efficient and fast acting optogenetic tools that could compete with alternative technologies. We therefore identified the rate limiting step involved in the photoactivated cellular mechanisms in E.coli to optimise its parameters. In order to evaluate the feasibility and desirability of our results we decided to conduct a Cost Analysis to evaluate whether it would be economical for a lab to implement our changes. The results obtained were passed on to our wet lab to minimise the range of values they would need to test in the lab.

OptoFlux

OptoFlux determines the optimum conditions required for our co-culture powered LIT bulb to produce a luminescence equivalent to a 160W conventional lightbulb. We are co-culturing Cyanobacteria with engineered E.coli cells, where Cyanobacteria photosynthesises to produce glucose for our luminescent E.coli. We understand that the light intensity will vary throughout the depth of our LIT bulb, and since light tends to be the growth-limiting substrate in photosynthetic processes we thought it would be important to optimize our lightbulbs dimensions, to ensure homogeneity and cell growth are consistent throughout the bulb. Through optimising the dimensions of our bulb we were able to build a 3D diagram of our LIT bulb on AutoCAD.

GOLIT (Game of LIT)

GOLIT is an adaptation of John Conway’s Game Of Life (1970). GOLIT is a cellular automaton that simulates the adhesion of cells, where a set of rules stimulate a change in a cells state over a period of time. The cells in GOLIT can either be in a bound or free state. The probability of a cell changing state depends on the number of neighbouring bound cells it has and the concentration of SpyTag/SpyCatcher, the binding partners that allow the cells to adhere to eachother, expressed on the surface of the cells. Since our designs are LIT, we decided to incorporate machine learning within our GOLIT simulation, to determine the optimum starting configuration of our cells that would require the minimmum amount of time for all the cells on a surface of a defined size to adhere to eachother.

LAME (Light Activated Mammalian Expression)

LAME outlines the steps involved in the photoactivation of genes in mammalian cells that would allow for tissue regeneration. It is important for medical devices to work in a rapid and highly percise manner, therefore with the use of LAME we attempted to maximise the rate at which our photocleavable linker is cleaved so that our gene expression can be activated rapidly. LIT decided to run a cost analysis for this model, where a greater focus was placed on creating an efficient and robust optogenetic tool rather than a cheap less-efficient one. We collected our results and passed them on to our wet lab team to o minimise the range of values they would need to test in the lab.

Selecting our Software

This summer we decided to run our models on two different softwares: MATLAB and Python. Both are high level languages that allowed us to create robust simulations.

Upon starting iGEM, everyone on the modelling team wanted to learn a new programming language. Python’s versatile applications and the ability to import packages that other programmers have developed make it easy to get started with new projects. Moreover, since Python is an open source software it is relatively easy to find resources online to help troubleshoot our codes. Since Python is a high-level language we didn’t require a lot of training to become familiar with the language. We organised a couple of tutorial sessions with our iGEM advisers and then jumped straight into working with it.

We also decided to use MATLAB for our models, as we were already familiar with this language from our engineering degrees. MATLAB is a powerful language with a very large built-in database of algorithms for image processing. This was particularly useful for the adaptation of the Game of Life.
