Optogenetic tools and their importance

The field of neuroscience developed the idea of optogenetic tools: genes for light-sensitive proteins are introduced into cells to control them. The variety of light-sensitive proteins motivated us to explore the applications of some optogenetic tools with the aim to inform the research community and the general public about their potential and advantages over other forms of controlling organisms.

Why use cells to generate structures?

  • cells span multiple length scales and can therefore be used to generate from microscopic to macroscopic structures
  • structures could self-heal and can be easily recycled by light-inducing cell death or polymer degradation
  • Allows the generation of structures in environments that humans have no direct access to or are life-endangering

Organic architecture

We chose to apply light control in architectural structures to reimagine the vision proposed in the essay ‘Organics’ by architect William Katavolos. Can we grow structures out of living organisms?

Bacteria can be engineered to generate sustainable and eco-friendly materials, such as bricks and bioplastics. Also, natural and artificial light are central components of the space that we inhabit.

Barchitecture combines these two advantages by implementing 2 biological light switches in a system to guide cell structuring into desired 3D structures.

How is that useful?

Considering the increasing frequency of natural disasters, having a cheap and immediate way to generate structures can provide shelter to people.

To infinity and beyond

We didn’t limit ourselves to applications on Earth. Currently, significant trade-offs are made for the amount of necessary materials and equipment to be transported in outer space due to monetary and fuel considerations. Our light-guided system can also be applied to bacteria in space to mine silicates and metal oxides from Lunar regolith. After the harvest, bacteria can be guided to form desired structures and bind biosilica to generate semiconductors and circuit boards.

Small scale structures

The production of small scale devices with medical applications also derives from our ability to grow structures from bacteria. Biopolymers, such as PHB, have a wide range of applications (agriculture films, safe materials for toys and packaging, feedstock additives). Via accurate light control, PHB-based medical implants can be produced, such as biocompatible veins and bronchus. See here the outcomes of our conversation with Blue Alpha - a company producing PHB from bacteria.

Bioluminescence and light switches

We aim to produce a more efficient, longer-lasting and reliable light bulb that does not require electricity, mechanical input or continuous nutrient input. To achieve this, we are developing a “LIT Bulb” comprised of E. coli cells that can detect sunlight changes and luminesce at night time. To guarantee a constant food source, the E.coli cells will be co-cultured with cyanobacteria that can continuously produce and secrete glucose.

Informal conversations

Our intention was to learn about her experience with bioluminescent bacteria and more importantly, about the human-centered aspects of such a product: aesthetics, use and safety.
We talked about the engineering aspects that she experimented with and started a conversation about what potential light-induced technologies have for the future of design and bioluminescence.

Creating kits, giving people the possibility to build their own bacterial powered light-bulb, would make this new concept more exciting and push more people to want to learn about the science behind bioluminescent organisms.
