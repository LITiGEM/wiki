The aesthetics of light technologies

Deploying a bacterial light-bulb in real life can face major challenges: will people find it attractive enough to be on the streets? Can the engineering requirements meet the aesthetic ones? Where do we make compromises between the 2?

Through our Instagram competition, reaching 78,000 followers, we wanted participants to consider relevant aspects of deploying a bacterial light bulb in real life and submit designs for our prototype.

Entries, a final 3D-printed version of the bulb and tips for future iGEMers.

Architects

To go from cells to cities, we contacted architects to understand the scales one would have to work on and what would be possible in the short and long term.

Our first conversation was with Richard Beckett, a practicing architect from the Bartlett School of Architecture. After discussing our vision for architectural structures made from bacteria, we were pointed in 2 directions:

  • Academic architecture, where a bacterial 3D printing system would be a tool to develop concepts and prototypes.
  • Real life applications, where structures made from biopolymers can form temporary shelters, structures for festivals and plastic frames to be combined with cement in traditional buildings.

These ideas lead us to get in touch with Rachael Armstrong – a former TED Fellow who explores the intersection of biology and architecture.

Our Skype call was beneficial in terms of thinking about the scales on which architects work in terms of both time and space. The feedback obtained in the meeting was very supportive of trying to use bacteria to form structures.

Blue Alpha

In the early stages of the Barchitecture application, we consulted Blue Alpha – a company that specializes in PHB production for a variety of applications. We gained insight into small and large scale applications and the market demand. All the information shaped the direction in which our technology would be headed and informed our decisions on the entrepreneurial front.

One important idea emerged from our conversation. We were advised that as PHB is a biocompatible polymer, a direction could be to produce PHB-based medical implants. Via accurate light control, we could 3D print small biocompatible implants, such as veins. However, we chose to focus on large scale structures. Our motivation was twofold: the very promising feedback obtained from practicing architects and the material properties of bacterially-produced PHB, which are less suitable for medical implants, as Blue Alpha informed us.

From ambient lights to street illumination

We explored old and new areas for implementing bacterial light bulbs: ambient lights and street illumination. We contacted Transport for London and established a conversation around what criteria such a new technology would need to meet to be deployed as street lamps on London’s roads. We gained relevant information that then helped tailor our mathematical modelling and entrepreneurship. Our conversation with Teresa van Dongen, who designed a bacterial ambient light, revealed what is useful for designers who start using synthetic biology.

ARUP - the construction colossal

How can the construction industry overcome the depletion of raw materials, the increasing need to create sustainable buildings and the need for rapid structure building in the case of natural disasters?

To explore how our optogenetics application can provide solutions in the area of construction, we contacted ARUP – one of the biggest construction companies in the world.

During the meeting, we discussed the science and the potential for our Barchitecture application. At the end, ARUP proposed that they could give 50 hours of pro-bono consulting to develop a more concrete design for Barchitecture. We then produced a concise document that explains in detail the aims of the project and the nature of the collaboration. Given the nature of big companies, time was an issue in taking the project to a design phase where sketches would be built.

Here is the document, from which future iGEM teams can see a way of tailoring documents to model their interactions with industry stakeholders.

European Space Agency

We wanted to extend the use of bacterial architecture from Earth to outer space. The initial premise was that taking large amounts of building material when colonising new planets would be inefficient. To know about the challenges of deploying our barchitecture technology in space, we contacted the European Space agency.

After discussing how biological light switches can shape architecture in space, very interesting outcomes came out:

  • We decided jointly with our bulb project to find a way for cells to survive in extreme environments. So, we characterised the application of the IrrE biobrick for these purposes, contributing to our Gold Medal criteria (link).
  • Research has focused on decreasing the mass of materials and electronics by engineering microbes that can print the necessary components. To this end, our biological light switches can be integrated into ‘growing’ electronics through light guidance.

For instance, Lunar regolith bio-mining of silicates and metal oxides is a current active area of research. Taking it a step further and engineering bacteria to not only harvest silicates and produce biosilica, but also form desired structures and bind this biomaterial could be used to generate silicon semiconductors and circuit boards.

UK iGEM 2017

Together with the Warwick and Westminster iGEM teams, we hosted the UK 2017 iGEM Meet-up. Besides the usual project presentations and talks, we tested an idea developed from the very start of the project: what tools should one use to choose an iGEM project?

At the start of our project, many ideas and projects were discussed, debated and pondered upon. What perhaps needed some more consideration, was how do we think about the project development in terms of ethical, safety and societal considerations? What directions should be pursued?

To see what a good way to engage in such a thought experiment would be, we organised debates between UK’s iGEM teams on synthetic biology matters. Topics ranged from whether synthetic biology should be merged with AI, to whether there should be a limit on what can be genetically engineered, to who should own the intellectual property of scientific developments.
