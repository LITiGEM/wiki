The Modelling of LIT

Our project explored how 3 types of biological light switches can be combined in innovative applications. Upon exposure to light, these light switches either activate or suppress cellular processes. If put together carefully, switches and cellular functions such as cell adhesion can lead to novel ways of thinking about organ printing, street illumination and architecture. To find out more about the applications, click here.

For us, mathematical modelling was a tool to look at our applications of biological light switches in new ways. We used our models to:

  • Prepare wet lab experiments and determine how effective biological light switches can be
  • Think about new ways of communicating the project
  • Design our final product for entrepreneurship

We also challenged the conventional way we make assumptions about parameters in various biological processes. To overcome under and overestimation of parameters, we used:

  • Sensitivity analysis
  • Genetic algorithms
  • Parameter sampling

GOLIT (Game of LIT)

The ultimate goal of synthetic biology is to recreate cells as cellular automata, to simulate life and explore new forms of artificial life. We extended the use of cellular automata to help people visualize our project better.

With cellular automata, cells behave according to a set number of rules over a period of time. We hypothesized that this will help people visualize the processes underlying the formation of 3D bacterial structures better and created a cellular automaton. On the technical side of mathematical modelling, we incorporated a genetic algorithm to help us understand how light should activate cells to create a structure from bacteria in the least possible time.

OptoFlux

We aimed to engineer a bacterial light bulb that serves as more than an ambient light. We identified a significant design consideration: light must reach our co-culture bacteria for nutrition purposes and traverse the bulb when it is produced by the bioluminescent E. Coli.

We combined mathematical modelling and integrated human practices to determine what our light bulb should look like. Our OptoFlux considered homogeneity and cell growth to determine the dimensions necessary to produce luminescence equivalent to 160W â€“ about the same intensity as a street lamp.

LEGIT (Light Expressed Gene Induced Transport)

We considered two potential advantages of our light switches over alternative gene activation tools in our designs: efficiency and speed. To create 3D structures from bacteria, our cells need to produce the most amount of surface display proteins in the least amount of time. Achieving this is dependent on using an optimal light intensity level for gene activation.

Our LEGIT model describes the steps involved in the formation of 3D structures with the use of bacterial cells. LEGIT allows us to see how different light intensity levels affect the quantity and rate of protein production. By also incorporating parameter sampling, our results informed our wet lab experiments to test specific levels of light intensity, expected to be most effective in building bacterial 3D structures.

LAME (Light Activated Mammalian Expression)

LAME outlines the steps involved in the photoactivation of genes in mammalian cells that would allow for tissue regeneration. It is important for medical devices to work in a rapid and highly percise manner, therefore with the use of LAME we attempted to maximise the rate at which our photocleavable linker is cleaved so that our gene expression can be activated rapidly. LIT decided to run a cost analysis for this model, where a greater focus was placed on creating an efficient and robust optogenetic tool rather than a cheap less-efficient one. We collected our results and passed them on to our wet lab team to o minimise the range of values they would need to test in the lab.
