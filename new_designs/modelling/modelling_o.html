The Modelling of LIT

Philosophy and exciting nerdy stuff

Our project explored 3 types of biological light switches. Upon exposure to light, these light switches either activate or suppress cellular processes. Put together, they can lead to novel ways of thinking about organ printing, street illumination and architecture.

For us, mathematical modelling was a tool to look at our applications of biological light switches in new ways:

  • Contribute to the preparation Wet Lab experiments
  • Conduct Cost Analysis of our optogenetic tools
  • Introduce experimental results to determine the robustness of our models
  • Design our final product for entrepreneurship

We also challenged the conventional way modellers tackle parameter optimization in various biological processes. To overcome under and overestimation of parameters, we used:

  • Genetic algorithms
  • Parameter sampling
  • Sensitivity analysis

GOLIT (Game of LIT)

The ultimate goal of synthetic biology is to recreate cells as cellular automata, to simulate life and explore new forms of artificial life. We extended the use of cellular automata to help people visualize our project better.

With cellular automata, cells behave according to a set number of rules over a period of time. We hypothesized that this will help people visualize the processes underlying the formation of 3D bacterial structures better and created a cellular automaton. On the nerdy side of things, we incorporated a genetic algorithm to help us understand how light should activate cells to create a structure from bacteria in the least possible time.

OptoFlux

We aimed to engineer a bacterial light bulb that serves as more than an ambient light. We identified a significant design consideration: light must reach our co-culture bacteria for nutrition purposes and traverse the bulb when it is produced by the bioluminescent E. Coli.

We combined mathematical modelling and integrated human practices to determine what our light bulb should look like. Our OptoFlux considered homogeneity and cell growth to determine the dimensions necessary to produce luminescence equivalent to 160W â€“ about the same intensity as a street lamp.

LEGIT (Light Expressed Gene Induced Transport)

We considered two potential advantages of our light switches over alternative gene activation tools in our designs: efficiency and speed. To create 3D structures from bacteria, our cells need to produce the most amount of surface display proteins in the least amount of time. Achieving this is dependent on using an optimal light intensity level for gene activation.

Our LEGIT model describes the steps involved in the formation of 3D structures with the use of bacterial cells. LEGIT allows us to see how different light intensity levels affect the quantity and rate of protein production. By also incorporating parameter sampling, our results informed our wet lab experiments to test specific levels of light intensity, expected to be most effective in building bacterial 3D structures.

LAME (Light Activated Mammalian Expression)

We proposed a new way of printing organs: light activates the transcription of any gene of interest to enable desired differentiation. In a medical context, such a process needs to be rapid and highly precise.

Similar to how bacterial cells form 3D structures, mammalian cell adhesion is dependent on optimal levels of light intensity. Our LAME model helped us mathematically describe the stages of gene photoactivation in mammalian cells, resulting in us knowing what range of values for transcription activation is more likely to provide informative results in the lab.
