Things to look for: genetic algorithms, cost analysis, sensitivity analysis.

We created cellular automata to help people visualize the processes underlying the formation of 3D bacterial structures using our optogenetic tools. We incorporated a genetic algorithm to determine the optimum light pattern one should shine onto our cells to create a structure as quickly as possible.

We modelled under what levels of light intensity could our cells produce the maximum amount of surface display proteins in the least amount of time to create 3D structures. By incorporating parameter sampling, our results could accurately inform our what light intensity levels are optimal for wetlands experiments in our Barchitecture application. Click here for a journey from light activation to displaying adhesion proteins on the surface of E. Coli

Our LAME model describes mathematically the stages of light gene-activation in mammalian cells, as such we determined the precise range of values that are most likely to provide optimal results in the lab.

To obtain a light intensity of 160W from our bacterial bulb, we modelled the shape and dimensions of the bulb mathematically. Our results allow for enough sunlight to go through it to reach the cells, while at the same time allowing for the bioluminescent bacteria to light their surroundings.
