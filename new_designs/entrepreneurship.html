Light Induced TechnologiesLTD

Bringing urban design and synthetic biology together

1807: London’s Pall Mall becomes the first street in the world to be illuminated by gaslight.

2057: the London Eye uses bioluminescence to impress tourists during night time.

At LIT, we develop applications for biological light switches – molecular devices that activate and suppress cellular processes. Our focus is on controlling cellular organisms that can transform our current ways of thinking about urban design.

Our first product is a sustainable bioluminescent light bulb. It uses the daily cycles of day and night to regulate its bioluminescence via a genetically engineered circuit. Making use of bacterial co-culturing, the bulb provides an eco-friendly, cost-effective and aesthetically pleasing solution that is sustainable for the long term compared to other alternatives.

The why and how

Currently, the world is in the process of rethinking 2 essential aspects of what our metropoles mean: energy consumption and the integration of nature into urban design – sometimes involving the idea of biomimicry.

In the UK, the “Europe Strategy 2020” aims to cut CO2 emissions by 20% before 2020. Consequently, city councils are requested to implement measures that will reduce their carbon emissions. Now, 85% of city councils dim or turn off the streetlights to save electricity costs and reduce their carbon foot print.

We see the implementation of new and better technology to satisfy the needs for private and public illumination as the only solution to the issue. Not only will our products help with the current worldwide endeavour of reducing energy consumption, but they will also make citizens more aware of what their surroundings are made of and how one must take care of that. The concept of the citizen can again be relived.

We chose to first focus on the prototype of a bacterial light bulb, after reviewing the potential of all our iGEM applications developed during the past couple of months.

Over the summer, we contacted and built connections with architects, designers, lawyers and public service institutions in London to go from summer project to start-up. Their advice has been extremely valuable to the practicality of our products, their real-life implementation, public reception and what steps need to be taken.

Where to next?

The next few years will involve a lot more R&D, but we’ll not keep things quiet. Since we already hosted a competition to crowdsource designs for the aesthetics of our bulb, we intend to repeat such events where we can get as much feedback as possible from people and integrate that in our technologies.

Product

Our bacteria-powered lightbulb detects changes of light intensity in its environment, allowing the genetically engineered circuit in the bacteria to encode for bioluminescence only during night time. Making use of bacterial co-culturing, the bulb provides an eco-friendly, cost-effective and aesthetically pleasing solution that is sustainable for the long term.

Why we think we can have an impact

At LIT we got inspired by a set of principles laid out tech investor and PayPal co-founder, Peter Thiel:

“The most valuable businesses of coming decades will be built by entrepreneurs who seek to empower people rather than try to make them obsolete.”

“The best entrepreneurs know this: every great business is built around a secret that’s hidden from the outside. A great company is a conspiracy to change the world; when you share your secret, the recipient becomes a fellow conspirator.”

“If your product requires advertising or salespeople to sell it, it’s not good enough: technology is primarily about product development, not distribution.”

Therefore, we organized our strategy in terms of developing an awesome product, empowering people and building strong connections with fellow ‘conspirators’.

To get from 0 to 1, one needs to assess the impact that one can have – in our case in urban design and public illumination. Marketing

We considered two potential advantages of our light switches over alternative gene activation tools in our designs: efficiency and speed. To create 3D structures from bacteria, our cells need to produce the most amount of surface display proteins in the least amount of time. Achieving this is dependent on using an optimal light intensity level for gene activation.

Our LEGIT model describes specific steps involved in bacterial cells forming 3D structures, allowing us to see how different light intensity levels affect the quantity and rate of protein production. By also incorporating parameter sampling, our results informed our wet lab experiments to test specific levels of light intensity, expected to be most effective in building bacterial 3D structures.

The culture at LIT

Intellectual Property

We love open-source and we believe that the future of synthetic biology lies in everyone doing it responsibly, transparently and with the best intentions. The culture at LIT is about enabling people to creatively innovate and not shut down ideas if no short-term benefit is apparent.

Still, as any starting company, one must play the game. To obtain funding and support, it is necessary to go from 0 to 1 – develop technologies that are at least 10x better than what is already available. To achieve this, protecting the intellectual property of our technologies is necessary. In the same way that Google made their TensorFlow software available for everyone to dabble with machine learning for free, we would like in the next few years to open-source the way biological light switches can be used to innovate in everyday life.

Perhaps naively, we started out by reaching to Glowee, a company that is focusing on integrating bioluminescence on the streets. Their response, maybe unsurprisingly, was of keeping information confidential.
