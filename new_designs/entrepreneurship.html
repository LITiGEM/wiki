Bringing urban design and synthetic biology together

1807: London’s Pall Mall becomes the first street in the world to be illuminated by gaslight.

2057: the London Eye uses bioluminescence to impress tourists during night time.

At LIT, we focus on developing applications for biological light switches – molecular devices that activate and suppress cellular processes. When combined cleverly, technologies such as architecture from bacteria and sustainable bioluminescent bulbs emerge. We see synthetic biology at the core of urban design.

Product

Our bacteria-powered lightbulb detects changes of light intensity in its environment, allowing the genetically engineered circuit in the bacteria to encode for bioluminescence only during night time. Making use of bacterial co-culturing, the bulb provides an eco-friendly, cost-effective and aesthetically pleasing solution that is sustainable for the long term.

Business Environment

We aimed to engineer a bacterial light bulb that serves as more than an ambient light. We identified a significant design consideration: light must reach our co-culture bacteria for nutrition purposes and traverse the bulb when it is produced by the bioluminescent E. Coli.

We combined mathematical modelling and integrated human practices to determine what our light bulb should look like. Our OptoFlux considered homogeneity and cell growth to determine the dimensions necessary to produce luminescence equivalent to 160W – about the same intensity as a street lamp.

Marketing

We considered two potential advantages of our light switches over alternative gene activation tools in our designs: efficiency and speed. To create 3D structures from bacteria, our cells need to produce the most amount of surface display proteins in the least amount of time. Achieving this is dependent on using an optimal light intensity level for gene activation.

Our LEGIT model describes specific steps involved in bacterial cells forming 3D structures, allowing us to see how different light intensity levels affect the quantity and rate of protein production. By also incorporating parameter sampling, our results informed our wet lab experiments to test specific levels of light intensity, expected to be most effective in building bacterial 3D structures.

Intellectual Property

We proposed a new way of printing organs: light activates the transcription of any gene of interest to enable desired differentiation. In a medical context, such a process needs to be rapid and highly precise.

Similar to how bacterial cells form 3D structures, mammalian cell adhesion is dependent on optimal levels of light intensity. Our LAME model helped us mathematically describe the stages of gene photoactivation in mammalian cells, resulting in us knowing what range of values for transcription activation is more likely to provide informative results in the lab.
