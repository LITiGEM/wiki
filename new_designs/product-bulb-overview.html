The future of illumination

Electricity is a very convenient energy source that has revolutionised means of illumination, transportation and communication, however it has numerous negative impacts on the environment and human health. The increasing production and usage of electricity has greatly contributed to the carbon footprint and the unnatural degree of global warming which in turn has affected numerous ecosystems and wildlife.

We aim to produce an efficient, longer-lasting and reliable light bulb that does not require electricity, mechanical input or continuous nutrient input. To achieve this, we are developing the “LIT Bulb” comprised of E. coli cells that can detect sunlight changes and luminesce at night time. To guarantee a constant food source, the E.coli cells will be co-cultured with cyanobacteria that can continuously produce and secrete glucose.

We decided to explore how one would deploy a bacterial light bulb in real life by combining mathematical modelling with integrated human practices and entrepreneurship to develop a prototype.

Mathematical Modelling

Our final aim for this application was to develop a prototype. We took an engineering perspective, by using mathematical modelling to determine the dimensions and shape of the bulb. The final results cater for bacteria producing an equivalent luminosity to standard bulbs.

Human Practices

Deploying a bacterial light-bulb in real life can face major challenges: will people find it attractive enough to be on the streets? Can the engineering requirements meet the aesthetic ones? Where do we make compromises between the 2?

Through our Instagram competition, reaching 78,000 followers, we wanted participants to consider relevant aspects of deploying a bacterial light bulb in real life and submit designs for our prototype. Entries, a final 3D-printed version of the bulb and tips for future iGEMers.
