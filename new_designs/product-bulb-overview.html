Science & Parts

Our bacterial light bulb faced many challenges from the start

After Glowee decided that theyâ€™re too cool for school, we reached to Teresa, a Dutch artist, who designed and produced a bioluminescent light-bulb with bacteria.

How does it work?

Our intention was to learn about her experience with bioluminescent bacteria and more importantly, about the human-centered aspects of such a product: aesthetics, use and safety.
We talked about the engineering aspects that she experimented with and started a conversation about what potential light-induced technologies have for the future of design and bioluminescence.

Creating kits, giving people the possibility to build their own bacterial powered light-bulb, would make this new concept more exciting and push more people to want to learn about the science behind bioluminescent organisms.

Mathematical Modelling

Our conversation with Teresa turned into 2 ideas worth pursuing throughout the project: the aesthetics and human-centered design of our technologies and information that could be beneficial to designers. So, we took these ideas further with our infographic for designers interested in using light-induced technologies and our Instagram competition in collaboration with @the_art_competition.

Light can excite cells. Can LIT do the same?

We strongly felt that the initial engagement, the very first step of science communication, needs a refresh. To test our assumption, we gamified the central dogma of molecular biology and observed what people thought of the idea and how we presented the idea of tissue engineering.

The result? A game installation at London Science Museumâ€™s Late event, attended by more than 5000 people. See here what gamifying molecular biology looks like, public perception and feedback.

A question of aesthetics

Deploying a bacterial light-bulb in real life can face one major challenge: will people find it attractive enough to be on the streets? Can the engineering requirements meet the aesthetic ones?

We decided to explore the design of our bacterial light bulb, combining mathematical modelling with integrated human practices and entrepreneurship to develop a prototype.

On the side of aesthetics, we ran and Instagram competition, reaching 78,000 followers, to encourage people to imagine the aesthetic design of the bacteria-powered light-bulb. We wanted participants to consider relevant aspects of deploying such a technology in real life and submit designs.
